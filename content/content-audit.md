### Script 1: "You're Not Broken" (15s)

**Score: 8/10**

| Category | Rating | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Hook strength | 9/10 | "You're not broken" lands immediately. It's a relief statement. She stops scrolling because she's been TOLD she's broken. |
| First 3 seconds | 8/10 | Strong hook + 1 second beat is solid. The beat risks losing impatient scrollers, but the text on screen covers it. |
| Curiosity gap | 7/10 | The micro question ("You know that voice?") works, but it's a known pattern. She's heard "that voice" framing before. |
| Pacing | 9/10 | Tight for 15 seconds. No fat. Every line earns its place. |
| Repetition problems | 6/10 | "You're not broken" appears twice in the first half. Intentional repetition, but the second time doesn't add a new layer. It just restates. |
| Emotional pull | 9/10 | "Being needed by everyone and chosen by no one. Including yourself." That's the line that gets saved. It's devastating. |
| Payoff quality | 7/10 | "She's just been waiting for you to stop fixing and start listening" is decent but slightly abstract. WHAT does listening look like? She's left wanting. |
| Ending strength | 7/10 | CTA "Comment 'waiting'" is fine. Not remarkable. |

**Weak spots:**

1. **"You know that voice? The one that says something is wrong with you because you don't feel the way you used to?"** This is two sentences doing one job. The second sentence over-explains the voice. She already knows which voice.
   - **Cut:** "The one that says something is wrong with you because you don't feel the way you used to?"
   - **Replace with:** Let the next line ("That voice is lying") hit harder by landing sooner. The gap between "You know that voice?" and "That voice is lying" is the power. Don't fill it with explanation.

2. **Second "You're not broken"** in the BUILD section restates without escalating.
   - **Replace:** "You're not broken. You're exhausted." becomes "You're exhausted." She already heard "not broken." Now give her the truth underneath it.

3. **"She's just been waiting for you to stop fixing and start listening."** Generic coaching language. "Stop fixing and start listening" is a platitude in this space.
   - **Replace with:** "She's been waiting for you to sit still long enough to hear her." More visceral. More specific to the woman who can't stop moving.

## PART 2: 30-reels-blogs-susan-davis.md

### GLOBAL ISSUES ACROSS ALL 30 SUSAN DAVIS REELS + BLOGS

Before individual scripts, here are systemic problems that weaken the entire set:

**1. Hook Monotony.** At least 12 of the 30 reels use the same hook structure: "[Statement about menopause/desire/hormones]. [Contrarian follow-up]." After she's seen three of these, the format becomes predictable. She stops being surprised.

**2. Professor Susan Davis is cited too often in reel scripts.** On Instagram, name-dropping a researcher mid-reel loses people. Authority works in blogs. In reels, it slows momentum. Keep Davis references in blogs. In reels, say "the research" or "the leading researcher on this" once, max.

**3. "But here's what the research/evidence/science actually shows" appears in nearly every script.** This is a crutch transition. It needs variety.

**4. Most reels are information delivery, not emotional experience.** The hooks promise feeling ("you're not broken"), but the scripts deliver education. For this audience, feeling comes first. Facts validate the feeling.

**5. Blog tone is consistent and strong but repetitive across 30 entries.** Every blog follows: anecdote/hook, "here's what the research says," Davis citation, reframe, warm close. After blog 5, the reader knows exactly what's coming. Vary the structure.

**6. Overuse of em-dash-like constructions.** Multiple scripts use "..." where the pacing intention is right but some use constructions that read as em-dash substitutes. Keep ellipses for genuine trailing-off, not as dash replacements.

### Reel 1: "Your Desire Didn't Break at Menopause"

**Score: 6/10**

| Category | Rating | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Hook strength | 7/10 | "Your desire didn't break at menopause." Decent reframe. But it's a statement, not a provocation. |

| Repetition problems | 3/10 | "Broke" / "broken" / "break" used in hook, mid-script, and close. It's the same word doing the same job three times. |

**Weak spots:**

1. **"But here's what the research actually shows... testosterone decline is age-related, not menopause-related. It's been dropping since your twenties."** This is a science lesson in a reel. She didn't come to Instagram to learn about testosterone timelines. She came because she feels broken.

**Blog: 7/10.** Solid information. Davis citation is well-placed. But the opening ("I hear this all the time") is generic and the closing question ("Does this reframe land for you?") is weak. End with a statement, not a question.

### 2. Hook Patterns Are Too Uniform

Across 70 scripts, the dominant hook structures are:
- **Reframe statement:** "X isn't Y. It's Z." (used ~25 times)
- **Contrarian:** "Stop doing X." / "X isn't what you think." (used ~15 times)
- **List promise:** "Three things..." / "Five conversations..." (used ~5 times)

Missing hook types that would add variety:
- **Confession:** "I need to tell you something I've never said publicly."
- **Challenge:** "I dare you to do one thing tonight."
- **Absurdity:** "You'd never tell your daughter to feel this way. So why are you telling yourself?"
- **Cold open story:** Start mid-sentence in a client story, no setup.
- **Direct address:** "You. Yes, you. The one who just faked a smile."

### 3. The Core 10 Scripts Are Strongest, But...

The core 10 (reel-scripts.md) are the most retention-optimised. They use curiosity loops, delayed payoffs, and micro questions. But the other 60 scripts don't consistently use these techniques. The gap between the core 10 and the rest is noticeable.

**Recommendation:** Apply the retention architecture from the core 10 to the top-performing topics in the other two files. Specifically:
- Add micro questions at the 3-5 second mark in every Susan Davis reel
- Add delayed payoffs to every Tantric/Vanessa reel (currently most deliver the thesis immediately)
- Add open loops to captions in at least 50% of all reels

### 4. Blog Strategy

The Susan Davis blogs are strong but repetitive. No blogs exist for the Tantric/Vanessa file. If blogs are planned for those topics, avoid repeating the same structure. Vary with:
- Q&A format ("The question I get asked most...")
- Myth-busting format (shorter, punchier, numbered myths)
- Client story format (anonymised, detailed, emotionally rich)
- Letter format ("Dear the woman reading this at midnight...")

### 5. CTA Weakness

Across all files, CTAs default to:
- "Comment [word] if..."
- "Save this."
- "Share this with a woman who..."
- "Tag your partner."

These work but are generic. Stronger CTAs:
- "Tell me: when did it start?" (drives long comments, which boost reach)
- "The first woman to comment gets a voice memo from me." (drives speed)
- "Describe it in 3 words. I'll reply to every one." (drives engagement)
- "Whisper this to yourself tonight: [line from the reel]." (drives emotional imprint)
- "Screenshot this. Text it to her. She needs it." (drives off-platform sharing)
