# Teaching + Zoom Breakout Exercises — Connecting to What You Want & Saying It Out Loud

**A companion teaching module for the Radiant Women's Circle**
**Built on Claire Zammit's and Alison Armstrong's work, in the voice of the "Become a Sexually Empowered Radiant Woman" course**

> This is the "why" and the "how" behind finding your needs and desires, and then
> the part most of us were never taught: how to actually say them out loud in a way
> that inspires, instead of a way that lands as a complaint.
>
> The arc of the teaching:
> 1. **Why you don't even know your own needs** — Claire Zammit's "invisible"
>    identity, and how we unconsciously train other people not to see us.
> 2. **Why saying it feels so hard, and how you're wired** — Alison Armstrong's
>    diffuse awareness vs single focus.
> 3. **How to ask in an inspiring way** — the clean request, appreciation first, and
>    receiving what comes.
>
> Use the teaching in the main room. Run the exercises in Zoom breakout rooms, in
> pairs. Timings and facilitator scripts are all here.

---

## Before we start: the three words we blur together

Most women use these as if they're the same. They're not. Naming the difference is
half the work.

- **Needs** — what you can't be well without. Rest. Safety. To be seen. To matter.
  Non-negotiable. When a need goes unmet long enough, you go numb or you go into
  protest.
- **Wants** — the specific, changeable things that meet a need. "I want an early
  night." "I want to be held when I get home." A want is a need wearing clothes.
- **Desires** — the deep, alive, sometimes scary pull. What you long for. Pleasure,
  aliveness, to be chosen, to be free. Desire is the compass. It's how life moves
  you toward what's yours.

You can't ask for what you can't name. So we name it first. Then we speak it.

## Part A — The teaching (deliver this in the main room, ~30-35 min)

### 1. The invisible woman — why you don't even know your own needs

Before we talk about how to *ask* for your needs, we have to talk about something
deeper. Most women in this room don't actually know what they need. Not because
they're shallow, because they've been made invisible. Often by their own hand, without
ever realising it.

This is Claire Zammit's work, and it's some of the most important teaching I know.

Claire, who created the Feminine Power trainings, tells her own story. For years she
felt chronically **invisible**. She'd walk into rooms, events, relationships, and feel
unseen. Passed over. Like the opportunities went to everyone else. And she assumed it
was happening *to* her.

Then she discovered the truth that changed everything. She wasn't invisible because of
bad luck or a cruel world. She was invisible because of **how she was showing up**. Her
unconscious way of being was to disappear her own thoughts and feelings and simply
mirror whoever she was with. She'd become a mirror. So when someone was with her, they
weren't experiencing *her*. They were just having a deeper experience of *themselves*
in the reflection she held up.

She had trained everyone around her not to see her. And she'd done it so automatically
she couldn't even feel it happening.

**Let that land in the room. This is the piece most women have never heard.**

Here's how Zammit maps it, and how I want you to understand it tonight:

- We carry an unconscious **identity**, a core story about who we are. "I'm invisible."
  "I'm not enough." "I'm too much." "I'm a burden." "I'm alone." Most of us picked ours
  up so young we don't experience it as a belief. We experience it as reality.
- That identity drives **how we show up** without our permission. If deep down you
  believe you're a burden, you'll shrink. You'll say "I'm fine" when you're not. You'll
  put everyone's needs before your own and call it love. You'll apologise for wanting
  anything at all.
- And here's the cruel loop: **your way of being then creates the very reality you're
  afraid of.** You believe you're invisible, so you disappear yourself, so people don't
  see you, so you feel invisible. You believe you're a burden, so you never ask, so your
  needs go unmet, so you feel like a burden for finally needing something. The belief
  builds the proof.

**You are not being overlooked by accident. You have, without meaning to, taught the
people around you exactly how much of you to see.**

We do it in a hundred small ways:
- We say "I don't mind, whatever you want" until they stop asking what we want.
- We answer "how are you?" with "good, busy" until nobody looks closer.
- We hint instead of ask, so no one ever practises meeting us.
- We give and give from empty, so we become the strong one who "doesn't need much."
- We're so tuned to everyone else's needs, reading the room, managing the mood, that
  our own needs never even make it to the surface of our own awareness.

That last one matters most for tonight. **You can be so focused on other people's needs
that you genuinely don't know your own.** It's not that your needs are gone. It's that
you've spent so long facing outward that you've never turned around to look. Zammit's
whole point is that the numbness is not a flaw in you, it's a strategy that once kept
you safe. And it can be undone.

The shift, in Zammit's work, isn't to try harder to be seen. It's to first **get
connected back to yourself**, to what you're actually feeling, sensing, wanting, and
then to **take responsibility for making that visible.** To stop waiting to be
discovered, and start choosing to show up as yourself. When you change how you show up,
you change what becomes possible. You stop being the mirror. You let them see you.

> Say this to the room, slowly: You are not invisible, my love. You've been practising
> disappearing for so long it feels like the truth. But it's a habit, not a fact. And
> tonight we start practising the opposite.

### 2. Why you went blank when someone asked "what do you want?"

So you've been facing outward, disappearing yourself, for years. On top of that, there's
how you're wired. Alison Armstrong spent years studying the difference in how women and
men are built, and one piece explains so much of our struggle here.

Women live in **diffuse awareness**. Armstrong calls the feminine default "gathering
mode." In gathering mode you're aware of everything at once. What could feed us, what
could heal us, what's missing, what needs doing, who's upset, what's not right. It's
a superpower. It's also why, when someone asks "what do YOU want," you get a hundred
answers and no clear one. You're aware of everyone's needs so completely that your
own signal got faint.

Men, by contrast, live more in **single focus**. Armstrong's image is the hunter. You
can't hunt and do six other things. So when you "share" a concern the way women share,
diffuse and detailed, he often doesn't hear a request in it at all. He hears
information. And then you're frustrated he "did nothing," and he's confused because,
to him, you never actually asked.

Neither of you is broken. You're wired differently. And the bridge is learning to turn
your diffuse awareness into one clear, spoken want.

> Say this to the room: You're not too much. You're not needy. You've just been
> gathering everyone else's needs for so long you lost the thread of your own. Tonight
> we pick it back up.

### 3. Nagging vs a real request

Here's the trap Armstrong names so well. **Nagging is asking the same vague thing over
and over.** It doesn't work, and it corrodes you both. A real request is completely
different.

A real request has three things:
1. **Specific** — he can picture exactly what you mean. "More affection" is not a
   request. "I'd love a long hug when you walk in the door" is.
2. **A by-when** — "tonight," "this weekend," "before Friday." Diffuse wants float.
   A request lands in time.
3. **Room for a yes or a no** — a request the other person can't decline is a demand.
   Demands breed resentment. Requests breed generosity.

### 4. The Great Ask — Armstrong's four steps

This is the gold. When something really matters, don't hint. Build it like this:

1. **Describe exactly what you need.** Don't assume he knows what "support" or
   "romance" looks like to you. Paint the picture.
2. **Say what it would give you.** "When you do that, I feel wanted. I soften. I come
   home to myself."
3. **Show what's in it for him.** Not manipulation, truth. "And when I feel wanted, I'm
   so much more open to you." Armstrong's homely example: she told her kids, "If you
   let mum sleep till seven, mum will be more patient."
4. **Ask the partner question:** *"What do you need from me to be able to give me
   that?"* This one line turns a request into a team.

### 5. Frustration is a gap, not a fact

Armstrong reframes frustration as the distance between your **expectation** and your
ability to **influence** the outcome. We're riddled with expectations, and expectations
breed more expectations. The problem is we hold them silently and then feel let down
when nobody meets a bar they couldn't see.

An unspoken expectation is a resentment in waiting. A spoken request is an act of
intimacy. Same want. Completely different outcome.

> The work isn't to want less. It's to move the want out of your head, where it curdles,
> into your mouth, where it can actually be met.

### 6. Appreciation is what makes it inspiring

This is the piece that turns "asking" into something that lights a partner up instead
of shutting them down. Armstrong's Appreciation Equation: **appreciation unlocks
generosity.** Noticing what someone does builds devotion and makes them want to give
more. Criticism does the opposite. It closes the hand.

So the inspiring way to talk about your needs isn't a list of what's wrong. It's:

- **Appreciation first.** Name one true thing he already does before you ask for the
  next thing. "I love how you always make the coffee. I'd also love..."
- **Give it in his currency.** Appreciation only lands if it's in the language that
  matters to *him*, not the one that matters to you. Words, touch, being told he made
  a difference. Learn his. And learn your own, so you can actually receive it when it
  comes.
- **Make him the hero, not the problem.** Deep in every partner is someone who wants to
  succeed at loving you. "I want to be kissed slowly tonight" invites him to win. "You
  never kiss me anymore" tells him he's already lost.

### 7. Receiving is half the skill

Armstrong is blunt about this: men want to be received. She half-jokingly calls it
"peacocking," a man wants to impress you more than please you. If you deflect every
gift, downplay every compliment, insist you're fine, you starve the very generosity
you're longing for.

And you can't ask for intimacy from an empty tank. Armstrong's "sexy tank fillers"
are the things that make you receptive in the first place: rest, connection, being
touched without an agenda, feeling appreciated. Her "pumpkin hours" name the honest
truth that sometimes you're too depleted to receive anything, and that's information,
not failure. Know which one you're in. Ask from the fullness, not the fumes.

### 8. Inspire, don't demand — how to let your man succeed at giving you what you want

This is the heart of Armstrong's work, and it's the piece that changes marriages. A
demand and an invitation can carry the exact same words, and land in completely
opposite worlds.

A **demand** says: *you should already be doing this, and you're failing.* It makes him
wrong. And a man who feels he's already failed you stops trying, because there's no way
to win. Criticism closes the hand.

An **invitation** says: *here's something that would delight me, and you're just the one
who could do it.* It hands him a way to be your hero. And Armstrong is clear on this:
deep in almost every man is the wish to succeed at loving his woman. He's not
withholding on purpose. Usually he genuinely doesn't know what would land, because we
never told him clearly, or we told him in complaint, which he can only hear as failure.

So here's how you inspire instead of demand:

- **Assume he wants to give.** Start from "he'd love to make me happy if he knew how,"
  not "I shouldn't have to ask." Your tone follows your assumption, and he can feel the
  difference instantly.
- **Ask for the action, not the mind-reading.** "Notice when I'm tired and help" is a
  test he'll fail. "Would you run me a bath tonight?" is a gift he can give. Specific
  and doable is a kindness to him.
- **Make it winnable, and let him win his own way.** Tell him what you want, then leave
  room for how. Armstrong's point about single focus: a man given one clear target will
  aim at it. Hand him ten diffuse hints and he freezes. And don't redo it if he does it
  differently. If you correct the gift, you kill the giving.
- **Then let him see it worked.** This is the fuel. When he does the thing, show him it
  landed. "That bath was heaven, I feel like a new woman." That's not flattery, it's
  feedback. Armstrong's Appreciation Equation again: a man who sees that he made you
  happy will move mountains to do it again. A man who never finds out, or only hears
  what was still wrong, quietly gives up.

**The loop that gets women what they want:** clear invitation → he gives → she receives
and shows it landed → he wants to give more. Most of us have the opposite loop running:
silent expectation → he misses it → she's hurt and critical → he feels like a failure
and withdraws → she gets even less. Same man. Same marriage. The only thing that changed
is how she asked and how she received.

> Say to the room: You are not too much for wanting what you want. And he is not a lost
> cause for not knowing. You've just never been shown how to hand him the win. That's
> what we practise tonight.

**The whole teaching in one line:** *Come home to yourself, get clear on the want,
appreciate first, invite instead of demand, make him the hero, and then receive what
comes and let him see it landed.*

## Part B — Zoom breakout exercises (partnered)

Run each in breakout rooms of **2** (trios if you have an odd number). Assign
**Partner A and Partner B** by whoever's first name is earlier in the alphabet, so no
one wastes breakout time deciding. Broadcast the prompt to all rooms as a message
before you open them, and set a Zoom timer so they hear the warning before rooms close.

Bring everyone back to the main room after each exercise for a short go-round. That
main-room witnessing is where the shift locks in.

---

### Exercise 1 — How I make myself invisible (breakouts, 12 min)

**Why:** to catch, out loud, the specific ways each woman disappears herself and trains
others not to see her, the Zammit invisible-identity work.

**Broadcast this to the rooms:**
> Partner A, answer these out loud to Partner B: "One way I make myself invisible is..."
> and "One way I've trained the people around me not to see my needs is..." Then finish:
> "The core story underneath it might be... (I'm invisible / not enough / too much / a
> burden / alone)." Partner B, just witness. No fixing, no reassuring. Then swap.
> 6 minutes each.

**Facilitator note:** this one can go tender fast. Remind them the listener's only job
is to witness, and that naming the pattern is not the same as being the pattern.

**Main-room go-round (3 min):** each woman shares one sentence: "The way I disappear is
..." Naming it in the circle is the beginning of undoing it.

---

### Exercise 2 — "Sharing" vs "Asking" (breakouts, 10 min)

**Why:** to feel, in the body, the difference between diffuse sharing and a clear
request, the Armstrong distinction.

**Broadcast this to the rooms:**
> Partner A, spend 2 minutes telling Partner B about something you wish were different
> in your life or relationship, the way you'd normally talk about it. Ramble, share, all
> of it. Partner B, just listen. Then Partner A, say the SAME thing again in one clean
> sentence that starts with "I want..." and includes a by-when. Partner B, reflect back
> only the clear request you heard. Then swap. (5 min each.)

**Main-room go-round (3 min):** "What did it feel like in your body when you finally
said the clean version?" Most will say lighter, exposed, more real.

---

### Exercise 3 — Needs, Wants, Desires ladder (breakouts, 12 min)

**Why:** to climb from a surface want down to the need and up to the desire underneath.

**Broadcast this to the rooms:**
> Take turns. Partner A names one thing you want (a want). Partner B asks three times,
> gently: "And if you had that, what would it give you?" Follow it down until you hit the
> real need and the desire underneath. (Example: I want a night off → to not be
> responsible for everyone → to feel held → the desire to be taken care of.) Write your
> bottom line down. Then swap. 6 minutes each.

**Facilitator note:** tell Partner B their only job is to ask the question and witness.
No advice, no "oh me too." Just hold the space.

**Main-room go-round (3 min):** each woman shares only her bottom line: "Underneath it,
what I really desire is..."

---

### Exercise 4 — The Great Ask, out loud (breakouts, 14 min)

**Why:** to rehearse Armstrong's four-step request in a safe room before saying it to a
real partner at home.

**Broadcast this to the rooms:**
> Partner A, build a Great Ask out loud to Partner B, who plays your partner:
> 1. Describe exactly what you want.
> 2. Say what it would give you.
> 3. Say what's in it for them.
> 4. Ask: "What do you need from me to be able to give me that?"
> Partner B, receive it warmly and answer step 4 in character. Notice how different a
> clean ask feels from a complaint. Then swap. 7 minutes each.

**Coach into the rooms if you can drop in:** no apologising, no "this is silly but." Say
it like it's allowed. Because it is.

**Main-room go-round (3 min):** "What surprised you about asking that way?"

---

### Exercise 5 — Appreciation first (breakouts, 8 min)

**Why:** to practise the tone that makes a request inspiring instead of critical, the
Appreciation Equation in action.

**Broadcast this to the rooms:**
> Partner A, tell Partner B (as your partner) one specific, true thing you appreciate
> about them, and why it matters to you. Then, only then, add one clean want: "...and
> I'd also love..." Partner B, notice how the appreciation opens you before the ask
> lands. Swap. 4 minutes each.

**Main-room close (2 min):** "Feel the difference between being asked after being
appreciated, versus being told what's wrong. That difference is the whole game."

---

### Exercise 6 — Demand into invitation (breakouts, 10 min)

**Why:** to feel, out loud, how the same want lands as a demand versus an invitation,
the Armstrong inspire-don't-demand distinction.

**Broadcast this to the rooms:**
> Partner A, say one thing to Partner B (as your partner) the demanding way first, the
> way it comes out when you're hurt: "You never..." / "You should already..." Notice
> Partner B's face. Then say the SAME want as an invitation that hands him the win:
> "I'd so love it if you would..." and, when he imagines doing it, show him it landed:
> "...and it would make me feel..." Partner B, tell A honestly which version made you
> want to give. Swap. 5 minutes each.

**Main-room go-round (2 min):** "Which version were you more used to saying? And which
one do you want to practise this week?"

---

### Closing the module (main room, 5 min)

Bring them back for a final claiming round, like the circle close:

> One at a time, unmute and finish this out loud: "This week, the one clean want I'm
> going to actually say is..."

The circle's response to each, together and warm: **"You're allowed."**

Then send the take-home:
> Every day this week, make one clean, specific, appreciation-first request of someone
> in your life. Watch what generosity comes back. You're teaching the people who love
> you how to succeed at loving you.

---

## Facilitator quick-reference

- **Room size:** pairs. Odd number, make one trio and give it a touch more time.
- **Always broadcast the prompt** to all rooms before opening them, and re-broadcast the
  swap instruction at the halfway mark, breakouts swallow verbal instructions.
- **Assign A/B by first name alphabetical** so no breakout time is lost deciding.
- **Bring them back after every exercise.** The main-room go-round is the integration.
- **Protect the witness role.** In every pairing, the listener witnesses, doesn't fix.
  Say it before you open the rooms.
- **Watch the clock but read the room.** If a round is alive, add two minutes and trim
  the go-round.
