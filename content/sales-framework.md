# Sales Framework — Straight Line Persuasion (Jordan Belfort applied)

A complete close-the-deal framework for Tarisha's 1:1 coaching and group
programmes. Built for women over 40 who are interested but stall at the
buy. Every piece below stays on the straight line from open to close.

**Product / service**: 1:1 coaching and group programmes for women in
midlife who feel disconnected from desire, intimacy, their body, or their
partner. Sessions, packages, and the cohort container.

**Target buyer**: women 40 to 60. Smart. Successful on paper. Quietly
exhausted. The spark is buried. She's tried therapy, books, retreats. She
knows something has to shift but she doesn't trust another programme to
actually move her.

---

## 1. The Open — 30 seconds to instant rapport and credibility

The straight line says: in the first 30 seconds she decides three things
about you. Sharp. Enthusiastic. An expert in her problem. If she doesn't
feel all three, she's already gone, even if she stays polite on the call.

**The opener (say it warm, not rehearsed):**

> "Hi, it's Tarisha. Before we go anywhere, I want to say something. I
> work with women every week who tell me the same thing you wrote in
> your application... that they're successful, they're loved, and
> they still feel like a stranger in their own body. So you're not
> the odd one out. You're actually the most common woman I meet. The
> question isn't what's wrong with you. The question is what we're
> going to do about it. Sound fair?"

**Why it lands:**

- "I work with women every week" = credibility without bragging.
- "You're not the odd one out" = instant rapport, she exhales.
- "What we're going to do about it" = takes the wheel, sets the line.
- "Sound fair?" = first micro-yes. The straight line has begun.

---

## 2. Five qualifying questions that surface the real pain

Don't pitch. Don't fix. Just ask. Each question moves her further down
the line. Listen for the words she repeats, those are the words you'll
sell back to her later.

1. **"Tell me what's actually going on. Not the polished version. The
   one you'd tell your best friend at 11pm."**
   *(Surfaces the surface story. Write down her exact phrases.)*

2. **"How long has it been like this? And what made now the moment you
   decided to reach out?"**
   *(Pain + urgency in one. "Now" is the most important word in sales.)*

3. **"What have you already tried? Therapy, books, retreats, anything?
   And what happened when you did?"**
   *(Identifies her objection bank before she raises it. Also tells you
   what she's already paid for, which sets her price ceiling higher than
   she'll admit.)*

4. **"If we sat here a year from today and nothing had changed, what
   would that cost you? Not in money. In your marriage, your body, the
   way your kids see you?"**
   *(Future pain. This is the question that makes her quiet.)*

5. **"And if a year from today this was actually handled... what would
   be different? What would you be doing, feeling, saying yes to?"**
   *(Future pleasure. She's now selling herself the outcome.)*

By question five, she's done most of your work for you. You're not
selling, you're confirming.

---

## 3. Presentation structure — connect solution to her exact pain

The straight line says: never present a generic offer. Present her
words back to her, then bridge to your solution. Three beats. That's it.

**Beat 1: Reflect her pain in her language.**

> "So what I'm hearing is... you're not broken, you're exhausted. You
> don't hate intimacy, you hate the pressure of it. And the part that
> scares you most isn't the marriage, it's the thought of another year
> feeling like a stranger to yourself."

*(Use her exact phrases. If she said "shut down," you say "shut down."
Not "disconnected." Her words. Always.)*

**Beat 2: Name what's actually in the way.**

> "The reason therapy and books haven't moved this is because they're
> working on the story in your head. What you're describing lives in
> your body. Until we work there, the story keeps rewriting itself."

*(One sentence diagnosis. Don't lecture. Don't teach. Just name it.)*

**Beat 3: Bridge to the offer in three lines.**

> "What I do is different. We work in three places at once. Your body,
> your nervous system, and the way you talk to your partner. It's a
> twelve-week container. Weekly 1:1s, voice access between sessions, and
> a small group of women going through it with you. By week four, most
> women tell me they've felt their body again for the first time in
> years. By week twelve, the conversation with their partner has
> changed. That's what you're buying. Not sessions. The shift."

Now ask: **"How does that land for you?"**

That question is the pivot to the close.

## 4. Top 7 objections and the responses that move her, not push her

The straight line rule: every objection is either a real concern or a
smokescreen for one of two true objections... she doesn't believe in
*you*, or she doesn't believe in *herself*. Loop back to certainty in
both, every time.

### Objection 1: "I need to think about it."

> "I hear that a lot, and I respect it. Can I ask, when you say think
> about it... is it the money, the timing, or whether you actually
> believe this will work for you? Because those are three different
> conversations and I want to have the right one with you."

*(Never argue with "let me think." Diagnose what's underneath it.)*

### Objection 2: "I need to talk to my partner / husband."

> "Of course. And I'd love for you to. Can I ask one thing first... is
> he the kind of partner who'd say yes to you investing in yourself, or
> the kind who'd push back? Because if it's the second, talking to him
> first is going to make this harder, not easier. A lot of the women I
> work with tell their partner *after* they've decided. They come home
> already different. He notices. That's the conversation that lands."

*(Reframe the conversation she's avoiding without dismissing him.)*

### Objection 3: "It's too expensive / I can't afford it right now."

> "I hear you. Let me ask you something. A year ago, what did you spend
> on things that didn't move this? Therapy, books, the retreat, the
> supplements? ... Right. So the question isn't whether you can afford
> this. You've already been paying for this problem. The question is
> whether you keep paying for it in pieces, or you pay once and have it
> handled. Which feels more expensive a year from now?"

*(Reframe cost as the cost of *not* deciding. Use her own numbers.)*

### Objection 4: "I've tried things before and they didn't work."

> "That actually tells me you're exactly the right person for this. You
> didn't give up. You kept looking. The reason those things didn't
> stick isn't because you're broken, it's because they were working on
> the wrong layer. You don't need more insight. You've got insight for
> days. You need a body that finally feels safe enough to want again.
> That's what we do here. Different layer. Different result."

*(Validate the pattern, then differentiate the offer.)*

### Objection 5: "I don't have the time."

> "Tell me about your week... Right. So here's what I'd say. You don't
> have time *because* of what we're talking about. The exhaustion, the
> over-functioning, the saying yes to everyone. The work we do gives
> you the time back. Women in week six tell me they're sleeping
> deeper, saying no easier, and getting an hour back a day they didn't
> know they were leaking. The question isn't can you fit this in. It's
> can you afford another year of running on empty."

*(Reframe: the problem *is* the lack of time. The work returns it.)*

### Objection 6: "I'm not sure this will work for me. I'm different."

> "Every woman I work with tells me that in the first call. And every
> single one is right... she IS different. That's why this isn't a
> course. It's a container built around you. We're not running you
> through a programme. We're meeting your body where it actually is.
> If you weren't a little sceptical, I'd be worried. The women who do
> the deepest work are the ones who walked in with their arms folded."

*(Make her uniqueness the reason it WILL work, not a reason to wait.)*

### Objection 7: "Can I start in a few months when things calm down?"

> "Can I be honest with you? Things don't calm down. That's the
> story we tell ourselves when we're not ready to choose. In three
> months you'll have three more months of this same conversation in
> your head, and the version of you starting then is more tired than
> the version of you starting now. The best time was a year ago. The
> second best time is today. What's actually in the way of starting
> now?"

*(Names the avoidance gently. No shame. Then asks the real question.)*

## 5. Three closes that create urgency without manipulation

The straight line close is never a trick. It's a clean ask after the
certainty is built. If she's a yes, these get her there. If she's a no,
these reveal the real objection so you can loop back.

### Close 1: The Assumed Next Step

> "Okay, here's what I'd suggest. Let's get you started this week
> rather than next, because the longer you sit with the decision the
> heavier it gets. I've got a Tuesday at 10 or a Thursday at 2 to do
> our first session. Which one works better for you?"

*(Two yeses, never a yes/no. Movement, not pressure.)*

### Close 2: The Cost-of-Inaction Close

> "Let me ask you the question I asked at the start. A year from
> today, if nothing changes... what does that cost you? ... Right.
> Now, the investment for the twelve weeks is X. The cost of another
> year of what you just described is not X. It's your marriage, your
> body, and the woman you used to be. I'm not telling you to do this.
> I'm asking you to compare the two prices honestly. Which one are
> you more willing to pay?"

*(Urgency from her own future, not from a fake deadline.)*

### Close 3: The Permission Close (the one that works on her specifically)

> "Can I tell you what I think is actually happening right now? You
> already know you want to do this. You've known since you filled out
> the application. The part of you that's hesitating isn't asking for
> more information. She's asking for permission to put herself first
> for the first time in a long time. So I'm going to give it to you.
> You're allowed. You don't need anyone else to sign off on this. It's
> your turn. Are you ready?"

*(This is the close for the woman who over-functions for everyone.
It works because it names what she's actually waiting for.)*

## How to use this framework

1. **Don't read it on the call.** Internalise it. Then talk like a human.
2. **Stay on the line.** If she wanders, ask a qualifying question to
   bring her back. Never chase tangents.
3. **Tonality over script.** Warm, certain, slow. Never pitchy. Never
   rushed. Belfort's whole system is tonality. Yours has to be hers.
4. **Loop, don't argue.** Every objection gets acknowledged, reframed,
   then closed again. Three loops max. After three, it's a no for now.
5. **A real no is a gift.** It frees you both. Thank her, end warm,
   leave the door open.

The straight line isn't pressure. It's clarity. She came to the call
because something in her wanted to be met. Your job is to meet her, not
to convince her.
