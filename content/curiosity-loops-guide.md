# Curiosity Loops Guide ... Tarisha Starr

**How to hold attention without clickbait. Applied across all reel and blog content.**

---

## The Five Tools (And When to Use Each)

### 1. Open Loops

**What it is:** Start a thought, delay the resolution. Her brain can't let go of an unfinished sentence.

**Where to place them:**
- After the hook (delays the reveal)
- Before the close (pulls into caption)
- Between sections in blogs (keeps scrolling)

**Plug-and-play lines (in your voice):**

| Placement | Line |
|---|---|
| After hook | "But that's not the part that surprised me." |
| After hook | "And what she said next changed how I see everything." |
| After hook | "I didn't expect what came after." |
| Mid-reel | "But here's the part nobody says out loud..." |
| Mid-reel | "And this is where it gets interesting." |
| Mid-reel | "Stay with me. This is the bit that matters." |
| Before close | "But there's one more thing you need to hear." |
| Into caption | "I'm going to share the first step in the caption." |
| Into caption | "What happened next is in the caption. It matters." |
| Into next content | "Follow. I'm going to say what no one else will." |
| Into next content | "This is part one. Part two is harder to hear." |

**Rules:**
- Only use 1-2 per reel. More than that feels manipulative.
- The loop must PAY OFF. Either in the same reel, in the caption, or in the next post. Never leave her hanging with nothing.
- Make the loop emotional, not informational. "What she said next..." is better than "Here are the 3 steps..."

---

### 2. Delayed Payoff

**What it is:** Promise something early, make her wait for it. Not long. Just enough to build a small ache of anticipation.

**Where to place it:**
- Between the hook and the reveal (build 2-3 layers of delay)
- Before the reframe (she expects it, but you add one more beat)

**How to layer delay naturally:**

**WEAK (no delay):**
"I asked her when she stopped choosing herself. She said 'years ago.'"

**STRONG (three layers of delay):**
"I ask my clients one question. Just one. It's not about sex. It's not about their partner. It's something much simpler than that. And much harder to hear. I ask: 'When did you stop choosing yourself?'"

Each layer ("it's not about X") eliminates what she expects, builds tension, and makes the actual answer land harder.

**Plug-and-play delay phrases:**

| Layer | Line |
|---|---|
| Elimination 1 | "It's not what you think." |
| Elimination 2 | "It's not about [obvious thing]." |
| Elimination 3 | "It's something much simpler. And much harder to hear." |
| Final delay | "She didn't say it right away. She waited. Almost like she was testing whether it was safe." |
| Pre-reveal beat | "And then... she said something I haven't stopped thinking about." |

**Rules:**
- Maximum 3 layers of delay. More than that and you lose her.
- Each delay must ADD something (emotion, specificity, tension). Not just stall.
- The payoff must be WORTH the wait. If the reveal is weak, the delay makes it worse, not better.

---

### 3. Micro Questions

**What it is:** Tiny rhetorical questions dropped into the middle of a thought. They work because her brain can't hear a question without trying to answer it. She's no longer passively watching. She's engaged.

**Where to place them:**
- At the 3-5 second mark (resets attention after the hook)
- After a heavy statement (gives her a beat to feel it)
- Before the turn/reframe (primes her for the shift)

**Plug-and-play micro questions (in your voice):**

| Type | Line |
|---|---|
| Self-recognition | "You know that feeling?" |
| Self-recognition | "Does that sound like someone you know?" |
| Self-recognition | "Have you ever thought about that? Really thought about it?" |
| Challenge | "So why does something feel so... off?" |
| Challenge | "But who holds you?" |
| Challenge | "When was the last time someone asked what YOU need?" |
| Invitation | "Do you hear what's underneath those words?" |
| Invitation | "What if I told you it doesn't have to be this way?" |
| Invitation | "What would change if you believed that?" |
| Reframe trigger | "What if the problem was never what you thought?" |
| Reframe trigger | "What if it was never about your body at all?" |

**Rules:**
- 1-2 per reel. They're powerful because they're rare.
- Ask questions she can't answer quickly. "You know that feeling?" works because she does, and she pauses to access it.
- Never answer your own micro question immediately. Leave a beat. Let her sit with it.
- In blogs: use them to break up long paragraphs. One question line between two paragraphs pulls the eye down the page.

---

### 4. Pattern Interrupts

**What it is:** An unexpected shift in pace, tone, volume, or direction that resets her attention. The brain is wired to notice change. When something breaks the expected pattern, she snaps back in.

**Where to place them:**
- At the 3-second mark (prevents the initial drop-off)
- After a heavy emotional build (prevents overwhelm scroll-away)
- Before the close (wakes her up for the payoff)

**Types of pattern interrupts (non-clickbait):**

| Type | How to do it |
|---|---|
| Volume shift | Go from speaking to whispering mid-sentence |
| Pace shift | Rapid delivery to sudden stillness |
| Tone shift | Heavy to tender (or tender to direct) |
| Physical | Lean in, lean back, close eyes for a beat |
| Directional | Stop talking about "her" and suddenly address "you" directly |
| Rhythmic | Build a list, then break it with a question |
| Expectation | "Stop trying to fix your sex life." (She expects tips. You redirect.) |

**Plug-and-play interrupt lines:**

| Context | Line |
|---|---|
| After a list build | "But who holds you?" |
| After heavy content | "But here's the part that changes everything..." |
| After softness | "I need you to hear this next part." |
| Mid-story | "And then she said something I wasn't ready for." |
| Before close | "What if you just... stopped?" |
| Tone flip | [Speaking normally] then [sudden whisper] "...she already knows." |

**Rules:**
- 1 per reel is enough. 2 maximum.
- The interrupt must feel like a natural shift in thought, not a performance.
- The best interrupts are emotional, not visual. A voice dropping to a whisper is more powerful than a jump cut.

---

### 5. Momentum Transitions

**What it is:** The bridges between sections that PULL her forward instead of letting her drift. Most content uses dead transitions ("Also..." "Another thing..." "Next..."). Momentum transitions create a small forward lean.

**Weak transitions (cut these):**
- "And also..."
- "Another thing is..."
- "On top of that..."
- "The next point..."
- "Moving on..."

**Strong transitions (use these):**

| Purpose | Line |
|---|---|
| Deepen | "But it goes deeper than that." |
| Reveal | "And here's what nobody tells you..." |
| Redirect | "But that's not actually the point." |
| Escalate | "And it gets worse. Or maybe... better. Depends on how you look at it." |
| Surprise | "But here's the part I didn't expect." |
| Permission | "And this is the part where everything shifts." |
| Intimacy | "Can I be honest with you about something?" |
| Urgency | "This next part matters more than everything I just said." |
| Bridge to caption | "I'll tell you what I told her in the caption." |
| Bridge to body | "But your body already knows this. She's been saying it for years." |

**Rules:**
- Every transition should create a micro-feeling of "wait, what?" or "tell me more."
- In blogs: use these between every major section. They're what prevent the reader from closing the tab.
- Never use the same transition twice in one piece.

## How to Apply Across All 60 Reels

### The quick audit checklist

For each reel script, check:

- [ ] **Does the hook open a question in her mind?** If not, add a micro question or implied mystery.
- [ ] **Is there at least one moment where she doesn't know what's coming next?** If not, add a delayed payoff or open loop.
- [ ] **Does something shift at the 3-5 second mark?** If not, add a micro question or pattern interrupt.
- [ ] **Does the transition into the reframe/turn PULL her forward?** If not, replace the bridge with a momentum transition.
- [ ] **Does the close open a loop into the caption or next content?** If not, add a single line that drives the next action.

### The 3-touch rule

Every reel should have exactly 3 curiosity touches. No more, no less.

**Touch 1 (seconds 0-3):** The hook itself IS a curiosity device. Make sure it opens a question, names a mystery, or creates tension.

**Touch 2 (seconds 3-8):** A micro question, pattern interrupt, or delayed payoff that resets attention after the initial hook.

**Touch 3 (final 2-3 seconds):** An open loop into the caption, a line that lingers, or a question she can't stop thinking about.

Three touches feels natural. One feels flat. Five feels manipulative.

### Before and after examples

**BEFORE (flat, linear delivery):**
"Testosterone isn't the magic fix they're selling you. The only solid evidence for testosterone in women is for HSDD. Not energy. Not mood. Not anti-ageing. If someone is telling you testosterone fixes everything, ask what data they're using."

**AFTER (3 curiosity touches woven in):**
"Testosterone isn't the magic fix they're selling you. [HOOK]
I know you've seen the posts. Take testosterone, get your spark back. But can I tell you what the world's leading researcher actually found? [TOUCH 2: micro question + delayed payoff]
The only solid evidence for testosterone in women is for one specific condition. That's it. Not energy. Not mood. Not anti-ageing.
So if someone is telling you testosterone fixes everything... the question isn't whether they're wrong. It's why they're saying it. [TOUCH 3: open loop, she now questions the motive]"

**BEFORE (blog, linear flow):**
"Your desire didn't break at menopause. Testosterone declines gradually from your twenties onwards. So what actually changed? Everything else."

**AFTER (curiosity loops woven in):**
"Your desire didn't break at menopause. [HOOK]
I hear this all the time. 'I hit menopause and lost my sex drive.' And I get it. It FEELS like that's what happened. But what if the timing was a coincidence? [TOUCH 1: micro question that challenges her assumption]
Here's what the science actually tells us... [TOUCH 2: momentum transition, 'here's what' creates lean-forward]
Testosterone doesn't suddenly drop at menopause. It's been declining since your twenties.
So what actually changed? [TOUCH 3: micro question as section break]
Everything else."

## Applying to Blogs (Long-Form Curiosity)

In blogs, the same principles apply but spaced differently:

### Section breaks
Every 2-3 paragraphs, insert a micro question or momentum transition. This is what prevents the scroll-away.

"So what does this actually mean for you?"
"But here's where it gets personal."
"And this is the part I wish someone had told me years ago."

### The opening paragraph
Start with the hook, then immediately open a loop. Don't resolve it until paragraph 3-4.

"I need to tell you something about your desire that's going to change how you think about yourself. But first, I want you to notice something."

### The closing paragraph
End with an open loop into the next piece of content, a practice, or a question that follows her off the page.

"Try this tonight. Put your hand on your belly. And ask her: what do you need? The answer might not come in words. It might come as a feeling. Or a memory. Or a tear. Let it come. And if you want to know what to do with what comes up... that's the next post."

---

## The Non-Clickbait Test

Run every curiosity device through these three questions:

1. **Does the payoff match the promise?** If you say "this changed everything," the thing that follows must actually be meaningful. Not a platitude.

2. **Would you say this in a real conversation?** If a friend told you this story, would the delay feel natural? Or would you say "just tell me already"?

3. **Does it serve HER or serve YOUR metrics?** Curiosity that helps her stay engaged with content that helps her = good. Curiosity that tricks her into watching content that wastes her time = clickbait.

If it passes all three, use it. If it fails any one, cut it.
