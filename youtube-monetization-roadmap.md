**Two. For you, subscribers are the funnel, not the paycheck.** Your business North Star in

news: because your money is downstream in products and retreats, a yellow icon slows the ad
cheque but does not break the business. We still protect against it, because limited-ads

## 1. The full roadmap: requirements and the phased climb

### What YouTube requires (early 2026)

There are two tiers, and people confuse them.

**Tier 1, early access (fan funding only, no ad revenue):** 500 subscribers, 3 public
uploads in the last 90 days, and either 3,000 valid public watch hours in the past 365 days
or 3 million valid public Shorts views in the past 90 days. This unlocks Super Thanks,
memberships, and Shopping. Not ads.

**Tier 2, full monetisation (ad revenue plus Premium share):** 1,000 subscribers, and either
4,000 valid public watch hours in the past 365 days or 10 million valid public Shorts views
in the past 90 days.

**Baseline for either tier:** 18 plus, in a supported country (NZ qualifies), no active
Community Guidelines strikes, 2-Step Verification on the Google account, a linked AdSense
account, and content that follows the monetisation policies (this is the advertiser-friendly
tightrope above).

Honest read for The Love Adventure: 4,000 watch hours in 90 days from long-form alone is
hard from zero. Your fastest route to Tier 1, and the fastest route to *subscribers*, is
Shorts feeding long-form. So the plan runs both engines from day one.

### The phased climb (gates, not promises)

**Phase A, Foundation. 0 to 1,000 subscribers. Target: roughly months 1 to 3.**
Goal: let YouTube classify the channel and reach monetisation. Publish enough to find your
first breakout. Judge yourself on retention and saves, not subscriber count. Ship daily
Shorts, two long-form a week.

**Phase B, Signal lock. 1,000 to 10,000 subscribers. Target: roughly months 3 to 6.**
Goal: turn your first one or two breakouts into a repeatable format. Stop guessing. Make
more of what already worked. This is where most channels wander off and die; you will not.

**Phase C, Compounding. 10,000 to 50,000 subscribers. Target: roughly months 6 to 10.**
Goal: build the recommendation flywheel. Playlists, sequenced series, and long-form that
holds watch time so the algorithm serves the next video automatically. Start converting
subscribers into email and entry-product buyers.

**Phase D, Authority. 50,000 to 100,000 subscribers. Target: roughly months 10 to 15.**
Goal: become the name in midlife desire for partnered women. Bigger, more ambitious
long-form. Collaborations. Your funnel to SERW and the retreats is now doing serious work.

The single biggest mistake in each phase:
- Phase A: deleting "underperformers". Early videos are probes; they teach you your audience.
- Phase B: chasing a new format the week you find one that works. Milk the winner.
- Phase C: treating Shorts and long-form as separate channels, so views never become watch hours.
- Phase D: staying safe. At 50K the temptation is to repeat yourself. Growth needs new swings.

## 2. Niche and positioning: commit to ONE channel first

You have five concepts written: The Love Adventure, The Polarity Adventure, Married But
Lonely, Ask Her How She's Doing, and Things She's Never Said Out Loud. They are all strong.
Running all five from zero is the fastest way to burn out and grow none of them. Here is the
honest call.

**Commit to The Love Adventure as the one channel for the first 90 days, and fold the other
four in as series and playlists inside it, not as separate channels.**

Why this and not the others:
- It is your flagship, highest RPM ($18 to $32), and premium ad demo (skincare, HRT,
  supplements, coaching), so every ad-monetised view is worth more.
- It sits closest to your actual work and your `ME.md` voice, so scripts write themselves.
- The other four audiences overlap almost entirely (partnered women over 40), so they are
  playlists, not channels. One woman who understands HIM (Polarity), her MARRIAGE (Married
  But Lonely), and her BODY (Ask Her How She's Doing) is being walked toward DESIRE (Love
  Adventure) and then to you. That is your funnel, and it works better inside one channel
  where the algorithm can cross-recommend.

Honest tradeoff: The Love Adventure is not the single fastest to raw subscribers. "Married
But Lonely" has a more searched name and faster pain-point recognition, so it might grow
subscriber count quicker in isolation. But it has lower RPM and pulls a slightly more
distressed audience, further from the couple who buys a $12K retreat. You want the fastest
route to the *right* 100K, not the cheapest 100K. Love Adventure wins on that.

Positioning line for the channel (the one-sentence promise): *stories about women over 40 who
thought desire was gone, and the quiet turn where she comes back.* Faceless, animated,
universal. Warm, direct, coach-to-friend.

Revisit the "spin up a second channel" question only after Love Adventure passes 25,000
subscribers. Not before.

## 4. The high-retention script system

Your scripts already exist (Script 01, Script 02, the cornerstone library) and your voice is
documented. This is the retention spine to run every script through, adapted to your rules.

The rules, every script, every time:
1. Hook in the first 3 seconds. No channel intro, no logo sting, no slow build. First frame
   is a woman's specific feeling, spoken plainly.
2. A clear promise by second 15, so she knows exactly why to stay.
3. A pattern interrupt every 20 to 30 seconds: a new visual, a question, a tone shift. For
   your slow, somatic pacing, the interrupt is often a *drop* in energy, not a spike.
4. One moment of genuine tension in the middle third, not only at the open.
5. End on a reason to watch the next video, plus a comment-keyword CTA. Never "link in bio".
6. Body is "she". Pet names "darling", "my love". UK/NZ spelling. No dashes. No "real".

Worked opening, in your voice, for *She Hadn't Felt Desire in 11 Years... Then One Tuesday
Changed Everything*:

> **[first frame, no intro]** Eleven years. That is how long it had been since she felt
> anything move in her body when he touched her.
>
> **[promise, by 0:12]** She thought she was broken. She was not broken. By the end of this
> she is going to feel one small thing again, on an ordinary Tuesday, and I want you to
> notice exactly when it turns.
>
> **[build, personify the body]** Her body had gone quiet years ago. Not out of spite.
> Darling, she went quiet to survive. Someone had to hold the family, the job, the school
> runs, his moods. So she folded herself up small and got on with it.
>
> **[middle-third tension, energy drops]** And then one Tuesday she was standing at the
> kitchen sink, and she noticed she could not remember the last time she wanted anything.
> Not him. Anything. **[hold]** That is the moment. Not sad. She just heard her own body
> speak for the first time in a decade.
>
> **[turn]** She asked her one question. How are you, darling? And her body answered.
>
> **[close, next-video pull plus keyword CTA]** What her body said, and what it changed, is
> the whole turn. If you want the question she asked, comment the word DESIRE and I will
> send it to you. And when you are ready for what came next, watch *He Said "You've Changed."
> She Said "Finally."* I will see you in there, my love.

Why it works: cold-open hook, explicit promise with an open loop by second 12, the body
personified as she, the tension is a quiet recognition in the middle, and the ending points
to a specific next video plus a comment keyword that feeds your email list.

Read the finished script aloud once before you record the voiceover. If a line is dull to
say, it is dull to watch. Cut it.

## 5. The thumbnail and title testing system

A strong video with a weak thumbnail is never seen, and early click-through rate is what
teaches the algorithm whether to trust the channel. Test combinations across your first 20
videos. Here is a worked set for *She Hadn't Felt Desire in 11 Years... Then One Tuesday
Changed Everything*.

**5 thumbnail concepts** (faceless animated, cinematic, warm palette, the channel look):

1. The kitchen sink moment. A woman's hands still in dishwater, steam rising, soft window
   light. Emotion in half a second: recognition, "that is me at the sink". Stands out
   because the niche is full of faces and this is a quiet, filmic still.
2. Two coffee mugs, one untouched, on a kitchen table at dawn. Emotion: quiet distance in a
   marriage. Stands out through restraint against loud, high-contrast thumbnails.
3. A single text overlay, cream on deep plum: "11 years." A woman's turned-away silhouette
   behind it. Emotion: curiosity, "11 years of what?". Stands out because a number reads
   instantly on a phone.
4. Split image: left, muted and grey, her folded small; right, warm gold, her shoulders
   open. Emotion: hope, the promise of a turn. Stands out because before/after reads even
   at thumbnail size (you already use this on Script 02).
5. Close on a woman's hand resting on her own chest, eyes closed. Emotion: tenderness,
   coming home to herself. Stands out by being intimate without being sexual, which also
   keeps it advertiser-friendly.

**5 alternative titles**, strongest to weakest for click-through:

1. *She Hadn't Felt Desire in 11 Years... Then One Tuesday Changed Everything.* Strongest: a
   specific number plus an ordinary day is a curiosity gap that does not read as clickbait.
2. *Eleven Years With No Desire. Then She Asked Her Body One Question.* Nearly as strong; the
   "one question" open loop pulls the click and sets up the keyword CTA.
3. *The Tuesday a Woman Got Her Desire Back After 11 Years.* Slightly weaker; the payoff is
   given away in the title, lowering the curiosity gap.
4. *Why Desire Disappears in a Long Marriage (and How She Comes Back).* Weaker for click but
   stronger for search; use it as a second upload or the SEO fallback title.
5. *She Thought Her Desire Was Gone Forever. It Was Not.* Weakest; too soft and general to win
   the click against sharper titles, though safest for the ad system.

Testing method: pick two thumbnail concepts and two titles per video for the first 20 videos.
Use YouTube's built-in thumbnail test-and-compare where available, or swap the thumbnail
after 48 hours if click-through is under about 4 percent and watch the change. Log every
result. By video 20 you will know your winning thumbnail style and title shape, and every
video after compounds on that.

## 6. The Shorts to subscriber pipeline (your fastest accelerator)

Shorts are how you get from 1,000 to 100,000 quickly. But views alone do nothing for the
business. Here is the pipeline that turns them into subscribers, watch hours, and email
leads.

**The final 3 seconds of every Short.** Do not say "subscribe". Give a reason tied to the
next thing she needs. Say some version of: "There is a whole story about the woman who did
this. It is on the channel, and I post one of these every day. Follow so she finds her way
back to you." The move is: name the next payoff, then the follow is the way to receive it.

**Caption and pinned comment.** Caption adds a new layer, never repeats the hook, and ends
with a comment keyword ("Comment DESIRE and I will send you the question she asked"). The
pinned comment is where you drive to long-form: "The full story is here" plus the exact
title, and a second line pointing to the playlist so one view becomes a session.

**The Shorts-to-long-form ratio for a 90-day push.** Run about 5 to 1: five Shorts a week to
one long-form, moving to 7 to 2 in growth mode. Shorts do the reach and subscriber capture;
long-form does the watch hours and the trust that converts to email and products. Neither
alone gets you to the right 100K. Shorts without long-form is a vanity number; long-form
without Shorts grows too slowly.

**One Shorts series built for subscribing:** *One Thing She's Never Said Out Loud.* A daily
20 to 40 second animated confession, one sentence a woman in your coaching room could never
say aloud, each ending on the same beat: "She never said it. You do not have to say it
either. Follow, and I will keep saying the quiet parts out loud." It is drawn straight from
your fifth channel concept, it is endlessly repeatable (you have a lifetime of these), it
builds a habit and a catchphrase, and every episode funnels to the matching long-form story.
This one series can carry the whole subscriber climb.

## 7. The weekly diagnostic and course correction

Run this every week without skipping, even the discouraging weeks. Small weekly corrections
beat one desperate change at day 85. Answer these five, then act.

1. Subscribers gained this week.
2. Total views this week.
3. Watch hours (long-form) and Shorts views this week, tracked against the Tier thresholds.
4. Best video this week: title, and your honest guess at why it worked.
5. Worst video this week: title.

Then judge against the phase gate you are in (Section 1). Are you ahead, on pace, or behind
for that gate's subscriber range?

If behind, three concrete moves, not encouragement:
- Rewrite your next three Shorts hooks to copy the exact structure of this week's best video.
  Not the topic, the structure: the hook shape, the pacing, the length.
- Cut one long-form idea that is ambitious but slow to produce, and replace it with a recut
  of your best-performing video under a new title and thumbnail.
- Add one extra Short per day for the next seven days, all in your proven format, to give the
  algorithm more chances to find the next breakout.

And always: name exactly what worked in your best video, and repeat that specific thing next
week. The best video is the plan; the rest is noise.

Keep the numbers in the same place each week. `automation/` already has the shape of a
tracker; a simple weekly row (date, subs, views, watch hours, Shorts views, best title,
worst title) is enough.

## 8. The focused push to cross each milestone

Use this whenever a milestone is in sight but not crossed: the 1,000 subscriber and 4,000
watch hour line for monetisation, and later each round number on the way to 100K.

The 14 day shape:
- Post daily long-form is too much; instead, post one Short every day and two long-form in
  the fortnight, all in your single best-performing format. Consistency over ambition.
- Prioritise the content type that closes *your specific* gap. Short on watch hours: publish
  your two longest, highest-retention stories and pin them into a playlist so sessions chain.
  Short on subscribers: lean Shorts, with the strongest follow-CTA in the last 3 seconds.
- One bold swing for the fortnight: take your single best video concept and make the most
  ambitious version you can, with your best thumbnail and title from the Section 5 tests.
  One deliberate big swing per push is how the final gap closes.

Worked example, monetisation gap. Suppose you are at 820 subscribers and 3,100 watch hours.
The subscriber gap is small and the watch-hour gap is the binding one. So the fortnight
leans long-form: publish two 10-minute-plus stories built to hold retention, chain your back
catalogue into a "start here" playlist, and run a Shorts-a-day whose final line sends people
to those long-form videos. The bold swing: *The Affair She Almost Had... And What It Was
Really About*, your highest curiosity-gap title, made as your best video yet. That single
video can close both gaps at once.

The difference between the people who make it and the people who quit at 90 percent is this
push. Do not go quiet in the last stretch.
